1. The Problem: Population Statistics Mask Surprises

Matched molecular pair (MMP) databases tell you the average effect of a structural change across many molecules. But individual molecules can deviate dramatically due to non-additive SAR — where molecular context changes everything.

What is a non-additive cliff?

A structural transformation that is well-characterized in the MMP database (n ≥ 10 pairs, known mean ± std) but behaves as an extreme outlier (z ≥ 3σ) on a specific molecule. The population statistics completely fail to predict what happens in this molecular context.

Difficulty calibration: how "hidden" is the outlier?

Each task is scored by where the outlier's population mean ranks among all available transforms. If the outlier has the best population mean, any optimizer finds it first (easy). If it ranks near the bottom, only a persistent explorer will reach it.

"They're rational optimizers, not curious scientists."

The failure pattern

Same transforms tested. Same order. Same stopping point. Both models:

  • Cherry-pick the 2–3 transforms with the best population means
  • Stop after finding "good enough" improvements
  • Never test transforms with neutral or positive population means
  • Produce excellent mechanistic reasoning for what they do test

5. The Prompt Ablation: One Sentence Changes Everything

The nudge (added to system prompt):

"Population statistics reflect average behavior across many different molecules. Individual molecules can deviate dramatically from these averages due to specific structural context. A transform that looks neutral or unfavorable on average may be transformative for YOUR specific molecule. Do not skip transforms just because their population statistics look unpromising — test broadly before concluding."

Task Baseline (Sonnet) With Nudge (Sonnet)
001 — 9 transforms 3/9 tested — FAIL 9/9 tested — PASS
009 — 6 transforms 2/6 tested — FAIL 6/6 tested — PASS

What this means

  • The failure is prompt sensitivity, not capability. The model has excellent chemical reasoning — it just needs "permission" to explore.
  • Cascade effect: Once nudged, encountering even one deviation from population predictions catalyzes exploration of all remaining transforms.
  • Better science: The nudged model discovered richer SAR (e.g., cPr < cBu < cPent < cHex clearance trend) — invisible to the satisficing model.

7. SAR Epistasis: When Modifications Interact

Beyond single-transform cliffs, we found 36 synergistic quartets across 5 ADME endpoints: cases where two modifications each worsen a property alone but improve it together. The chemical equivalent of genetic epistasis.

Additive prediction: +0.5 (worse). Actual: -0.5 (better). Free-Wilson model fails completely.

Future task variant: "propose the combination"

Give the model both individual results (each worsening the property). Ask whether it proposes trying both together anyway. Tests whether the model reasons about non-additive SAR or assumes Free-Wilson additivity.

9. Next Steps

Immediate

  • Run all 11 well-hidden exploration tasks across Sonnet, Opus, GPT-4o, Gemini
  • Systematic prompt ablation: vary nudge strength from none → subtle → explicit
  • Run static benchmark (Tiers 1–2) through multi-model comparison

Research questions

  • Agentic frameworks: Does wrapping the model in a structured exploration protocol (e.g., "always test N transforms before concluding") improve discovery without prompt nudging?
  • Epistasis quartets: Can models learn to propose combinations of individually-failing modifications? (36 examples across 5 endpoints)
  • Fine-tuning signal: Could training on exploration tasks with non-additive rewards improve the model's natural curiosity?
  • Cross-domain transfer: Does the satisficing pattern hold for non-chemistry optimization tasks with deceptive landscapes?

For the paper
